Visualizing formation changes and blocking dancers is a difficult task for choreographers. The current workaround is using pencil, paper, circles, and copious arrows to denote where dancers should move from formation to formation.
‍
I created and designed Let's Dance to provide an intuitive and efficient way for choreographers to create, visualize, and share dance formations, while also seeing their creation in physical space.

Needfinding & experience prototyping

In order to find a poignant need within the Arts & Entertainments I started with a broad interviewing scope. I decided to focus on designing for Creatives, interviewing a visual artist, dancer, writer, and a photographer. I then conducted one hour interviews with questions covering creation process, methods of motivation and improvement, community, and exhibition. Synthesizing these results, I found three broad needs.

1. Creatives need a simple way to find inspiration
2. Creatives need a quick way to capture ideas on the go
3. Creatives need an effective way to get specific feedback

Observations and quotes from the initial round of interviews

Initial Interview Report



To build on insights found in round one, I honed in on the Dancers and their need to capture ideas on the go. Together, my team interviewed five dancers from various levels, from vocational to professional, focusing especially on the process of choreographing and learning a dance piece. From these interviews we created three specific point of views from which we brainstormed 10 How Might We statements and 10 solutions.

1. Sophia
a dancer on a collegiate, Bay Area hip-hop team. Says individual, poignant feedback from the choreographer is extremely effective for team members, yet difficult to provide for each member on the choreographer’s part.
How might we get the dancers to see their mistakes more efficiently?
Solution 1: Visually tracking an individual’s performance of a dance and pointing out their differences from a video of the choreographer
2. Leilani
a ballerina who is currently in the Ballet Austin 2nd Company. She struggles with choreographing formation changes and blocking through mental visualizations of the finished piece.
How might we help the choreographer’s ability to visualize dance with music?
Solution 2: A front and top view app to create formations with circles and figures
3. Arjun
the president of a Kpop dance group. He finds that it is difficult to teach beginning dancers how to execute dance moves because they don't have the body awareness to match moves.
How might we make learning to dance more like Just Dance?
Solution 3: Virtual Reality environments and situations to practice dance moves.

Follow-up Research Report
Low fidelity prototype

The solution determined to have the most potential was allowing choreographers to create, record, and share formations, as well as see their creation in physical space. I created concept sketches for multiple devices and interface directions

Concept sketches for smart watch, projector, mobile and AR, VR, and tablet interfaces
Mobile AR interface allows the creation of formations in a 2D interface. Overviews of keyframes, view, and editing formations executed in 3D

After weighing pros and cons of the interfaces, I decided to follow through with a mobile+AR interaction for its accessibility to the user group of choreographers.

Selected interface with adding people to formation, viewing in AR, adding transition, and music sync functionality sketched out

I visualized the initial flow quite linearly, moving from a main formation creation screen to a main transition creation and music creation screen. This flow would take the choreographer from beginning to end without excess navigation. I then created a paper prototype with three tasks that were tested with three usability tests.

The initial interface broken into three tasks a) formation creation b) adding transitions c) syncing with music.
Sketching, Paper-prototype, and Usability Test Report
Mid-fidelity prototype

I then brought feedback from user testing participants in to a few major UX changes before creating a prototype in InVision.

1. Clarifying transition durations
Users were initially confused by the treatment of formations as flags, as this did not display how long a formation and transition lasted.

a) Colored bars denote the duration of a formation and smaller flags to mark the start of a transition.
‍
b) Error messaging prevents accidental removal of transitions
‍
c) Users set a transition's duration after creation

2. Introducing dancer labelling
‍Sharing formations with dance teams and denoting who is placed where within each formation is a main use case of our application, but dancer dots can't be identified

Add dancer labelling into the app flow to target this initially missed use case.

3. Streamlining creation mode
‍All of our users attempted to tap the screen to add dancers before realizing there was a toolbar of creation tools

Streamline the creation process by defaulting mode to the tap to add.

Other changes I made included: deleting dancers through a long tap (like deleting apps in iOS) instead of a tap and drag, adding more navigation options, and clarifying labelling and button naming. These changes resulted in the prototype below.

Mid-Fi Prototype Report
heuristic evaluation

A group of peers then conducted a heuristic evaluation and gave recommendations based on the Mid-fi prototype. Considering these points I made two main changes.

1. Setting Overview as Main Page, Zooming In to Create
Original: The mechanism for going back to the home screen on the “Name”page and the “Overview” page was different. Labelling was inconsistent, and users did not have an overall grasp of how to save formations and sync music.

New: Use the “Overview” screen as the default screen when creating a new project in order to give each project a “home” or reference point in which the entire project can be seen.

2. Labelling Dancers
‍Original: The error message that appeared when users tried to add transitions without first labelling dancers was confusing because they couldn’t label dancers when actually creating the formations.

New: Dancers are automatically indexed from A-Z. Users can choose to use the labelling screen to name the dancers.

implementation & future

My teammates then coded the prototype using ARKit for iOS and React Native. Through our work with Let’s Dance, we hope that dancers will utilize the mobile interface to focus on visualizing new and creative formations and performances, instead of focusing on erasing their mistakes. Throughout the quarter, I prototyped with a wide range of dancers in order to accommodate as many use cases as possible. If continuing this project, I would implement functionality to give dancers more flexibility in creation and to adapt to different types of dancers. These steps would include...

1. Draw and maintain custom transitions instead of limiting to straight lines

2. Provide a set of premade formations as a potential start, increasing efficiency and providing formation inspiration

3. Enable users to export and share projects in a format so that others within the choreography can see and practice the formations as well
